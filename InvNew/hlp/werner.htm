Werner Deconvolution

Werner (1953), analysing the magnetic field of arbitrary dikes, proposed a method for it’s interpretation. This method deals with 2-D problems. By solving the systems of linear equations we can find, either, under assumption of a thin dike object from D T curve it’s upper edge, or under assumption of a thick dike, from ¶ (D T)/¶ x curve its upper edges. Although the simplification seems to be a limitation, the method does provide a wide range of applications. Advantages and limitations of the process of inversion will be discussed in this chapter.

Assume an interference of more dikes. If we want to get some meaningful solution, we need to add to the interpretation equation a function that approximates the field of the “interfered” dike within the k-point operator window. What kind of function are we to use? Probably it can be only a polynomial function suggested by Werner, because the rational function affects the left-hand side of the equation (4.14) (isn’t there plenty of choices?). The second question is: what kind of the polynomial degree are we going to pick? Polynomials of the second, third degree can good approximate an interfered curve only in surrounding parts of an anomaly. Hence, we had better seek for a solution, which gives a better approximation, what is the higher order polynomial. One could bother, that such an approximation can have large amplitudes between the data points, but we need a good approximation in data points. Unfortunately there will be numerical problems in practical computing. The system of linear equations will be ill conditioned; its condition number will be very high ( ). Such a systems of linear equations do not have solutions in a sense of classical numerical analysis. One possible way is to use regularisation.

Sensitivity to the noise: Recall the equation (3.48) of the condition number and the chapter about SVD. From discussion about SVD we can see that small error either in measured data b or coefficients of the matrix A can cause unacceptable error in the solution. The condition numbers of the systems of linear equations solved in Werner deconvolution are ~104 – 1030 (ideal is 1), what means that the systems are ill conditioned. For this reason small perturbations may cause that the solution fails.
